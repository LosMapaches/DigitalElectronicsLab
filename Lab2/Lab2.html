Introduction:

This laboratory explores the effect of probe compensation versus non-compensation. Basically, probe compensation for an oscilloscope allows for the rise time of an input voltage to be faster. This is important for high speed measurements. However, there is a considerable attenuation of the signal. This attenuation factor can be calculated for and compensated for by the equipment. This is why some oscilloscope probes have the options for 1x, 10x, and/or 100x compenation for the signal attenuation based on probe charcateristics. Basically, the signal gets multiplied by a factor to display what the signal should be. Probes allow for change in internal capacitance for adjustment of rise time. Modifing the internal capacitance can yield overcompensation, undercompensation, or correctly compensated readings. Shown below are examples of different compensated signals.

A relationship for attenuation for the signal can be determined for a probe. The appendix (Pages 3 and 6) shows releveant circuit calculations. For a 10x compensated probe, the output voltage should be 1/10 of the input voltage. This is why a mulitple of 10 is applied for the compensated probe used in this laboratory. The attenuation is a result of trying to increase the responce time of measurment devices.

The delay time was defined in Lab 1: Spice/RC Circuit for Digital Signal. The following relationship can be used to define the delay time theoretically:


t=-RCln(0.5)


In the previous equation, R represents the resistance, C is the capacitance, and t is the delay time. We can use this calculation to attain capacitance from a measured delay time and resistor in an RC circuit.

Procedure:

The variable capacitance of a probe was altered to view the effects of overcompensation, undercompesation, and correct compensation. Additionally, a square wave with a peak-to-peak voltage of 2V and a frequency of 1 kHz was used to find the delay time for a compensated versus non-compensated probes. Delay times for the compensated probe was also determined for maximum and minimum delay time settings with respect to altered capacitance. An RC circuit containing the probe as the capacitor in series with a 98.3 kilo-ohm resistor was used to measure the capacitance of the compensated probe experimentally.

Discussion:

It was determined that the use of a compensated probe decreases the delay time compared to use of a regular wire. It was determined that the delay time was decreased by 5.89 micro seconds. The capacitance of the probe was also measured at minimum and maximum compensation capacitances. These values were compared to capacitances attained by measuring the delay time in an RC circuit. The difference in values can be attributed to parasitic capacitance in the experimental setup.
